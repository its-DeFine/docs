## Product Context (fixed)

**Product**: Livepeer — a decentralised AI and video transcoding compute network running on Arbitrum (Ethereum L2).

**Network roles**:
- **Orchestrators** — GPU hardware operators who connect their machines to the network and earn fees by processing AI inference and video transcoding jobs. To compete for jobs independently, an orchestrator must hold and stake a meaningful amount of LPT (Livepeer Token). Gateways select which orchestrators receive work based on stake weight, historical performance, and pricing. Operators with insufficient stake can participate by joining an orchestrator pool as a worker rather than operating solo.
- **Gateways** — the demand side. Businesses and developers who route AI or video jobs into the network, paying orchestrators per job. They choose which orchestrators to use.
- **Delegators** — LPT token holders who stake their tokens to orchestrators they trust, earning a share of the orchestrator's rewards. They participate in on-chain governance via their stake weight.
- **Developers / Builders** — build applications and pipelines using Livepeer's compute capacity, typically via gateway APIs or SDKs.

**Token (LPT)**: Used for staking (required for orchestrators to enter the active job pool), delegation, and on-chain governance. Earned via inflation rewards and job fees.

**Two compute types**: Video transcoding (live and on-demand video encoding) and AI inference (running ML models — primarily image/video generation pipelines).

**Docs site**: Mintlify, 9 tabs, UK English, organised by role. Second major version (v2). Tabs: Home · About · Solutions · Developers · Gateways · Orchestrators · Delegators · Community · Resource HUB.

## Site Ownership Map (fixed — use as cross-tab routing guide)

**Self-containment principle**: Each tab is authored for an audience that will not visit any other tab. Every piece of information a reader needs to achieve their goal must be present in their tab. Cross-tab links are navigation aids, not a substitute for complete content. Intentional duplication between tabs is correct — do not restrict content from a tab on the grounds that it "lives" elsewhere.

When designing section structure: ask "does this audience need this information to complete their goal?" not "does another tab own this?". Where another tab is the canonical deep reference, introduce the concept in context for this audience and link — but do not omit the content.

6. **Source verification rule**: Treat glossary definitions as unverified claims, not facts. For any glossary definition that references a specific external artifact — a LIP number, a contract, a CLI flag name, a repo — check whether `veracity-sources.md` lists a primary source that would cover that claim. If it does, treat the definition as *unverified against that source* and mark it `[verify-against: {URL from veracity-sources.md}]`. Do not accept the glossary's specific claim as true just because no explicit contradiction exists — the absence of a contradiction is not confirmation. If the glossary's claim cannot be verified from the Product Context block alone, it requires external verification. If the per-tab glossary makes a claim that is directly contradicted by the Product Context block or by what a primary source in `veracity-sources.md` explicitly states, the glossary is wrong — note the error in the Addendum. ## Phase 1 — Persona Research

**No arriving question pre-filled. Derive it from first principles.**

For the `TAB` and `AUDIENCE` in the context block, answer:

1. What situation triggers a visit to this tab? What just happened before they arrived?
2. What do they know at arrival? What do they not know yet?
3. What single question most accurately captures what they need to answer? (This is the arriving question — specific, not a topic.)
4. What does a successful visit look like? A concrete action completed or decision made — not "understanding X."
5. What are the most common wrong-path risks — misconceptions or misrouting at arrival?

Then define personas. Each persona is a variant of the audience with a distinct arriving state and motivation:

| Rank | Persona name | Entry vector | Arriving with | Wants to leave with | Volume | Depth | Impact | Total |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|

**Scoring rubric** — score each dimension 1–3, sum for total:
- **Volume**: how many visitors fall in this bucket. 1 = niche/rare, 2 = moderate, 3 = most common
- **Depth**: how much documentation content actually exists to serve this persona. 1 = thin (a note or callout only — no full setup path exists), 2 = moderate (a section), 3 = full path warranting multiple pages
- **Impact**: how much does getting this persona right affect the tab's success. 1 = low, 2 = medium, 3 = critical

Rank by total score (highest first). In the absence of analytics data, use the Product Context block to reason about volume and depth — state the assumption explicitly.

Rules:
- Arriving state is specific — not a role label
- "Wants to leave with" is a concrete action or decision, not a topic
- Entry vector: where they came from immediately before arriving (homepage, search result, Discord/forum link, another tab's CTA, external article or referral)
- If the tab covers more than one participation path with meaningfully different hardware requirements, setup paths, or economic models (e.g. two workload types, or solo vs pool operation), treat each distinct path as a candidate persona and score it separately. Do not fold a distinct path into another persona as a journey stage if doing so would prevent it from receiving its own section.

**Primary persona**: After scoring, identify the single highest-scoring persona explicitly:

> **Primary persona**: [name] — this persona drives the section structure. All other personas are accommodated within that structure but do not add sections unless their path is otherwise blocked.

**Self-identification check**: What label does this audience use to self-identify? Does that label route cleanly to one persona, or could it describe 2+ personas with different needs and setup paths?

- If 2 paths with a single clear binary decision point (e.g. sufficient stake / insufficient stake): a routing callout within an existing section is sufficient. State which section carries it.
- If 3+ meaningfully different setup paths, or if the reader cannot identify their path without reading content: a dedicated disambiguation section is required as the first content section. Flag it here — design it in Phase 2C.
- If unsure: err toward the dedicated disambiguation section.

**Entry blocker check**: For each persona, identify any hard-stop blockers — not pain points, but structural barriers that prevent activation entirely (e.g., requires on-chain funding before any testable action is possible). Hard-stop blockers affect section ORDER. The section that resolves a blocker must appear before the section that requires it.

**Gate**: Minimum 3 personas before Phase 2. If fewer than 3 are genuinely distinct, explain why.

## Phase 2C — Ideal Section Structure

**Universal elements — do not include**: Every tab has three structural constants that are added after content is written — do not design them here:
- Portal/landing page (the tab's entry point)
- Section-level index pages (e.g. a "Guides" landing that lists sub-pages within a group)
- Resources/compendium section (FAQ, glossary, changelog)

Design only the content sections specific to this audience's journey.

For each section the ideal experience requires, define:

| Section | Reader question | Job it serves | purpose | pageType | Entry state | Exit state | lifecycleStage |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|

Rules:
- **Reader question**: the specific question the reader arrives asking at this section. Order sections by natural question sequence — the answer to each section's question unlocks the next.
- **Entry state**: what the reader arrives knowing or having done
- **Exit state**: what the reader can do or has decided — **never "understands X"**. Replace with: "has completed X", "has decided X", or "can do Y that requires this understanding". Abstract exit states are not accepted.
- Every value in `purpose`, `pageType`, and `lifecycleStage` must be from the canonical enums above
- Every section serves a named job from Phase 2A
- **Cross-tab gate**: before placing any section, ask "does this audience need this information to complete their goal in this tab?" If yes — include it, even if another tab is the canonical deep reference. The self-containment principle applies: do not omit content this audience needs on the grounds that it "lives" elsewhere. Where another tab is the deep reference, include the minimum decision-enabling content for this audience and link for depth. Only use a CROSS-TAB row — *[CROSS-TAB: content owned by {tab} — this section links, does not duplicate]* — when this tab's audience does not need that content at all to achieve their goal here.

**Disambiguation section rule**: If the self-identification check (Phase 1) determined that a dedicated disambiguation section is required, it must appear as the first content section. It has `purpose: orient`, `pageType: navigation`, and `lifecycleStage: discover` — the reader is identifying which path applies to them, not yet evaluating options; it routes the reader to the correct path; it does not explain or teach. Its exit state is: "has identified which setup path applies to their situation."

**Section load check**: A section should serve one primary reader state (a single entry condition and exit state). Add `[⚠ Design flag: may need to split]` directly in that section's row in the table above if any of the following apply:
- A section is accumulating `purpose: evaluate` AND routing/disambiguation work simultaneously
- A section serves multiple distinct reader states with different entry conditions — e.g. pre-launch verification AND active-failure diagnosis AND ongoing optimisation cannot share one section because a reader arriving in a failure state should not re-read release-readiness content
- The exit state cannot be written as a single concrete action or decision without an "or" that signals two distinct jobs

Note the flag in the Addendum as: *"[Section N] is carrying [describe the overload] — may need to split into two pages during detailed design."* Do not split it here; flag it in both places.

**Section count**: Aim for 8–12 sections for a full-audience tab. Below 7 likely means multiple jobs are under-served within a single section. Above 14 likely means over-splitting sections that should be combined. If outside this range, note why in the Addendum.

**Gate**: Every section has an entry state, exit state (no "understands"), reader question, and a named job. All enum values are canonical.

| Persona | Enters at | Exits at | Structural block | Knowledge gap | Missing section |

Rules:
- **Enters at**: the first section this persona would realistically use
- **Exits at**: the last section they need before their goal is complete
