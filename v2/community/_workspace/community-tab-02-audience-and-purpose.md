## 1. The Purpose of This Section

The Community tab is the network's **participation infrastructure** — the place that converts a visitor's interest or curiosity into an active role in the Livepeer ecosystem. Every other tab has a functional purpose (build, operate, stake, govern). The Community tab's purpose is connective: it answers the question every cross-role participant eventually asks — *"How do I get more involved?"* — and routes them into the right structure to do it.

Specifically, this section:

- **Orients** newcomers to how this community is structured, governed, and engaged — the Foundation, workstreams, SPEs, channels, and norms
- **Activates** participants who want to contribute code, documentation, translation, governance, support, or ideas — with funded pathways to do so
- **Connects** community members to the surfaces where Livepeer actually happens — Discord, Forum, events, GitHub, X — without replacing those surfaces
- **Retains** existing participants by keeping them informed: roadmap, active governance, upcoming events, and ecosystem developments

It is not a links dump. It is not a social media aggregator. It is the narrative and structural layer that makes the network feel like a community rather than a protocol.

## 2. The Audience for This Section

Unlike the technical tabs (GPU Nodes, Gateways, Developers), the Community tab has **no single primary audience**. It is inherently multi-persona — the only tab that is legitimately so. However, "multi-persona" does not mean "undifferentiated." It means the section must route each persona efficiently to what they came for.

**Primary audiences:**

- **Newcomers to Livepeer** — crypto-native or not, evaluating whether to participate
- **Delegators / LPT holders** — already staked or considering it, looking for community connection, governance guidance, and peer resources
- **Active community members** — already participating, looking for contribution opportunities, events, funded pathways, or news
- **Orchestrators and GPU operators** — technically active, seeking community intel, governance context, and peer networks
- **Developers and builders** — looking for contribution pathways, grants, or community support channels
- **Governance participants** — active or aspiring, needing the governance map, SPE process, and GovWorks connection
- **External observers and researchers** — evaluating the ecosystem from outside

## 4. Purpose of Visit — What They Need and Want to Achieve

### 4.1 The Curious Newcomer
**Questions:** What is this community? Who is in it? How does it work? Is it active? Is it welcoming?  
**Wants to:** Understand the structure of the community, find the right channel to lurk or join, and form a first impression of community health.  
**Achieve:** A confident first step — joined the Discord, found the right forum category, or bookmarked the events calendar.

### 4.2 The Active Delegator / LPT Holder
**Questions:** Where are people talking about delegation and orchestrators? How do I vote? What is being discussed now? How do I access Live Pioneers? What is this GovWorks thing?  
**Wants to:** Stay informed, participate in governance, and connect with the delegator community.  
**Achieve:** Voted on an active proposal, joined a community call, or found the Live Pioneers app.

### 4.3 The Governance Participant
**Questions:** How does governance actually work? How do I submit an SPE? What is a workstream? Who is GovWorks? How do I draft a proposal?  
**Wants to:** Understand the governance process end-to-end and either vote or submit something.  
**Achieve:** Voted on a live proposal via Explorer, or started drafting an SPE with GovWorks mentorship.

### 4.4 The Potential Contributor
**Questions:** What can I contribute that isn't code? How do I contribute docs? Is there funding available? What are RFPs? How are contributors recognised?  
**Wants to:** Find a specific contribution pathway that fits their skills — and ideally one that has funded opportunities.  
**Achieve:** Submitted a PR, joined a workstream, or applied for a grant or RFP.

### 4.5 The Existing Orchestrator
**Questions:** What is the community talking about? What governance votes affect my earnings? Who else is running orchestrators? What tools does the community have?  
**Wants to:** Stay current with ecosystem developments and governance that affects their operation.  
**Achieve:** Found the relevant governance thread, bookmarked the orchestrator community channel, or discovered a community tool.

### 4.6 The Ecosystem Observer / Researcher
**Questions:** How large is this community? How active is governance? Who funds development? Is the Foundation legitimate?  
**Wants to:** Assess ecosystem maturity and community health.  
**Achieve:** Formed a credible picture of the network's community infrastructure.

## 5. Why They Want This Outcome

- **Newcomers:** Belonging and relevance. Decentralised networks are socially intimidating. The first experience determines whether they stay or leave. They want to feel like there is a place for them and that the community is accessible.
- **Delegators:** Economic alignment and confidence. Governance directly affects their staking returns. They are not passive — they are economically motivated participants who want to protect and grow their stake through informed participation.
- **Governance participants:** Influence and impact. They believe the network should develop in a particular direction and want their voice to carry weight in that development. The process is the barrier; lowering it is the unlock.
- **Contributors:** Recognition and reward. Contribution without recognition burns people out. Contribution with funded pathways and visible acknowledgement creates long-term engagement. The DePIN token model is an additional motivator — contributors can also be token participants.
- **Orchestrators:** Competitive advantage and operational intelligence. Community is a source of peer intelligence — pricing signals, governance outcomes, tooling improvements, and network developments that affect their operation.
- **Observers:** Due diligence and credibility assessment. A well-documented, active community is evidence that the network is healthy and low-risk to engage with.

## 6. The Process to Achieve Their Desired Outcome

### Newcomer process:
1. Understand what this community is and who is in it
2. Identify which role is closest to their situation
3. Find the right entry channel for that role
4. Take the first action (join Discord / Forum / events)

### Delegator / governance process:
1. Understand the governance model (on-chain voting, quorum, window)
2. Find where proposals live (Forum + Explorer)
3. Understand SPEs and workstreams (funded participation structure)
4. Take action: vote, comment, or draft
5. Optional: connect with GovWorks for proposal mentorship

### Contributor process:
1. Understand what types of contribution are valued (code, docs, governance, support, translation)
2. Find the relevant pathway for their skill type
3. Identify funded opportunities (grants, RFPs, workstreams)
4. Take the first contribution action (PR, Forum post, Discord join)
5. Get recognised / connected to the right team

### Orchestrator / participant staying current:
1. Find community-specific channels and tools
2. Locate governance discussions relevant to their role
3. Stay informed via news, events, and ecosystem updates

## 9. Voice Style for This Information

The Community tab has a different voice register than the technical tabs. It is not instructional. It is not referential. It is **invitational and directional** — it orients people and moves them forward.

**Appropriate voice styles by content type:**

| Content type | Voice |
|---|---|
| Orientation / overview | Concise, warm, factual — no hype |
| Governance explainers | Clear, factual, structured — civic register |
| Contribution pathways | Practical, action-forward, outcome-first |
| Channel directory | Descriptive, scannable, no superlatives |
| Events / news | Light, timely, accessible |
| Community guidelines | Direct, inclusive, non-punitive in tone |
| Roadmap | Forward-looking, narrative, measured optimism |

**Voice prohibitions for this tab:**
- No exclamation marks on factual content
- No "thriving community" / "passionate contributors" / "amazing ecosystem" language
- No passive descriptions ("this page can help you...") — active and outcome-forward only
- No Web3 jargon without immediate plain-language context

## 10. Preferred Information Delivery

Different sub-audiences have different consumption preferences, and these must be matched to content type:

| Persona | Prefers | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Newcomer | Scannable cards, short prose, clear hierarchy | Overwhelmed, orientation mode, not committing time yet |
| Delegator | Short facts, direct links, numbered steps | Action-oriented, knows what they want |
| Governance participant | Structured explainers, process flows, tables with parameters | Needs precision — mistakes here have financial consequences |
| Contributor | Categorised pathways, outcome statements, direct CTAs | Wants to know what to do, not read theory |
| Orchestrator | Links + context, not deep explainers | Already operating, visiting for efficiency not education |
| Observer | Narrative context + data points | Forming a credible picture, wants signals not marketing |

**Overall delivery principles:**
- **Segment over prose** — community information is multi-threaded; separate threads cleanly
- **Short explainers, not encyclopaedias** — concepts like SPE and workstream need 2–3 sentences, not 2 pages
- **Links as first-class content** — a correct link to the Forum governance category is more valuable than a 500-word description of what the Forum is
- **Timeliness signals** — indicate where information changes frequently; point to live sources, don't duplicate them
- **Progressive disclosure** — portal / overview entry → concept explainer → action page. Three levels, not one flat list
